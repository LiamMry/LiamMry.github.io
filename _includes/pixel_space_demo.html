The observation y (top-left, fixed) keeps only a random subset of noisy pixels. The other three cells are independent samples from p(x | y): each is a plausible completion, and they differ precisely where y leaves the image unconstrained — gaze, expression, hair texture.
Posterior — motion deblurring
Clip unavailable ffhq_posterior_motion_blur.mp4
Here y is a motion-blurred face. Three independent posterior samples recover the sharp image; they disagree only on the fine detail the blur destroyed — the posterior spread adapts to how much information y actually carries.
Faces make the mechanism visible — in practice I apply it to tomographic reconstruction across very different fields:
Fluid mechanics
Cross-section of a turbulent jet, reconstructed tomographically for aerospace research.
Medical imaging
Low-dose CT: fewer photons mean less radiation for the patient, but far noisier measurements.
Nuclear imaging
Emission tomography of nuclear material, where measurements are scarce and safety-critical.
